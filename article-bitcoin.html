What is Bitcoin, really? A Friendly Guide

Bitcoin is a digital currency, or cryptocurrency, that was invented in 2009 by a person (or group) using the name Satoshi Nakamoto. It was designed to let people send money directly to each other over the internet — no bank needed.

Unlike traditional money, Bitcoin isn't printed. It's "mined" by computers solving math problems and added to a public list of transactions called the blockchain. That public list keeps track of every Bitcoin movement in the world.

Only 21 million Bitcoins will ever exist, which means it's limited — kind of like digital gold. This scarcity is one reason people see Bitcoin as valuable.

Bitcoin is also decentralized. No single company, bank, or government controls it. That means no one can change the rules by themselves or freeze your account.

To store your Bitcoin, you use a digital wallet. And just like with an email address, you can receive Bitcoin using a public address — but only you can send it using your private key (your digital password).

Bitcoin can feel overwhelming at first, but at its core, it's just a way to send value — from one neighbor to another — safely and securely.
